During my PhD I used thermal quantum field theory and applied it to real (crystalline) materials to predict observable quantities.

"In computer science we are all standing on each others shoulders", is a quote from Paul Allen in his book "Idea Man". This is particularly true in materials theory, since many computational tools (i.e. software) already exists. My main research-expertise lies in the analysis of *interacting* systems, which requires the prior determination of various material constants through such software. To this end, I wrote the GRIT-code ([Gr]een-Functions for [I]nteracting [T]hermodynamics) that provides an interface to the Quantum Espresso code, the Wannier90 code, the Wannier-Tools code and the EPW-code and allows for further post-processing.

Something that I would be really interested in finishing, but (at the moment) dont want to make the time for, is the continuation of my material-superconductivity-predictor-pipeline:

A single "button" (metaphorically speaking; it will be a script that interfaces a variety of software-packages) that computes for an arbitrary material the resistivity as a function of temperature (and pressure). In particular: It computes at which temperature a material *can* become superconducting (by determining whether non-trivial solutions to Eliashberg-like-equations exist) and whether this state is energetically favorable (by comparing the grand canonical potential of the superconducting state with that of the normal state).

I believe that in my book-project I laid out many of the tools that are neccessary to accurately describe superconductors from first principles; However... It is a big project and pursuing it (by, for example, going after an academic career) seems incompatible with the stability that I seek for me and my family.
